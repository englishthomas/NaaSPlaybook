1 Introduction

Field Maintenance refers to the teams that perform on-site interventions. Field Maintenance responsibilities include, amongst others:

  • Corrective field intervention
  • Preventive field intervention
  • Spare parts management

While the Field Maintenance stream of the Playbook addresses the tactical and operational aspects of the tasks performed by the Field Maintenance Team, this module focuses on the corresponding management aspects.

This module presents an introduction to the functions, processes and systems that operate within the Field Maintenance team, and establishes guidelines for the NaaS Operator to define the structure of the Field Maintenance team, customize the processes that will be implemented, identify the tools that will support the operation, and select the implementation model to be followed along with the correct dimensioning of the Field Maintenance team.


1.1 Module Objectives

This module will immerse the NaaS Operator into the functions and processes of the Field Maintenance team and will provide the necessary directions to establish and manage the NaaS Operator Field Maintenance operations. The specific objectives of this module are to

  1. Provide an introduction to the Field Management team, providing guidance to the NaaS Operator to define the Field Maintenance basic structure that matches with NaaS Operator's requirements and capabilities.
  2. Enable the NaaS Operator to establish the core processes to be implemented for Field Maintenance.
  3. Provide a high-level view of the tools that support the functions and processes of the Field Maintenance team, enabling the NaaS operator to identify the required tools to be implemented in their organization.
  4. Provide guidelines to assess the Field Maintenance implementation model, including the right sizing of the team.

2 Field Maintenance Fundamentals

This section provides a general overview of the Field Maintenance team, including the organization structure and the scope for each team highlighting the relevance and potential impact for NaaS Operators.


2.1 Field Maintenance Overview

Before diving into the overview of the Field Maintenance team, the term Maintenance will be reviewed. Maintenance is often referred to as the actions executed with the objective of retaining or restoring equipment to a state in which it can perform its required function. These activities ensure that production systems and equipment operate consistently in a steady state of defined functionality and performance.

The Field Force team, also known as Field Maintenance or First Line Maintenance, is the team responsible for all on-site activities on the network. Field Technicians are the eyes, ears, and hands of the Network Operation Center at any site, performing activities like:

  1. Assisting troubleshooting on-site
  2. Fixing hardware-related events
  3. Connecting power plants or generators in case of commercial outage scenarios
  4. Validating site conditions
  5. Granting access to equipment to external service providers such as fiber company technicians.

The Field Force team is in charge of executing both corrective and preventive field interventions. Corrective interventions are the set of tasks performed to identify, isolate, and rectify faults on a failed equipment, which are commonly sent to Field Technicians via incident tickets created by the Network Operation Center organization. Preventive interventions are a set of tasks performed with a certain pre-defined periodicity (also referred to as scheduled or planned maintenance) to maintain equipment in satisfactory operating conditions, detecting and correcting incipient failures before they occur.

Field Maintenance is a crucial team on a network, as they serve as the vehicle to restore any on-site failure (corrective interventions) and to ensure optimum performance and prevent problems before they arise (preventive interventions). They are also in charge of spare parts handling and replacements performed at the site, when applicable. The NaaS operator must consider this team as a critical factor for their operation, since an efficient field workforce will enable increased business productivity and improved reputation by decreasing the turnaround time for repair services.


2.2 Field Maintenance Organization

From a functional view, the Field Maintenance team includes the following functions:

  1. Field Technician Activities
  2. b) Dispatcher & Coordination Function

Both functions will be covered in depth in Section 3 of this module, where guidance will be provided for the NaaS Operator to define the structure that best matches the intended scope of the organization.

From a process view, the Field Maintenance team includes the following responsibilities:

  1. Field Intervention
  2. Spare Parts Management
  3. Preventive Maintenance

These processes will be covered in depth in Section 4 of this module. The NaaS operator will be presented with alternatives to customize the processes according to its requirements and capabilities.

The teams working within the Field Maintenance organization coordinate their efforts with the Network Operation Center in order to prevent or minimize any unplanned interruption on the network or a reduction/degradation in the quality of the service delivered to the end-user. While the end-to-end flow will be presented in Section 4, a high-level overview of the interactions of the teams is presented below:

  1. Network surveillance is recorded by the Network Operation Centers Fault Management Tier 1 team and escalated to the Field Maintenance team if on-site assistance is required
  2. Field Coordinator/Dispatcher assures the ticket or work order is accepted by the Field Technician, and supervises travel time and distance
  3. Access Management is invoked when field assistance is needed and access to premises is required
  4. Field Technician performs activities on-site to rectify the fault and validates results back with the Network Operation Center
  5. Preventive maintenance tasks are planned, agreed, scheduled and executed between the Network Operation Center organization and the Filed Force organization.

3 Field Maintenance Functions

This section presents the definition of each function executed by the Field Maintenance team, focusing on its vital aspects and the configuration possibilities for the NaaS Operator.


3.1 Field Maintenance Functions Overview

The subsections below provide a drill-down into the functions of the Field Maintenance team, analyzing the key success factors and relevance/need for each function:

3.1.1 Field Technician Activities

The Field Technician activities function is the most vital function that is supported by the Field Force organization. Field Techs take over site assistance on sites, commonly for 24 hours a day and 365 days a year (although other arrangements do exist, as 8x5, 16x5, etc.), performing tasks to resolve faults on-site or assist with on-hands support in troubleshooting efforts along with the Network Operation Center's Tier 1 or Tier 2 engineers. The most common setup is to have a Field Technician crew consisting of at least two field techs working on a site at any given time; this is to facilitate work at height and for health & safety concerns in remote areas. However, the NaaS Operator may consider to have only one technician on site for simple preventive maintenance tasks that do not require work at height. From this point forward, every time a Field Technician is mentioned on this document it is translated to a Field Technician crew.

Field Technician function typically includes the following activities:

  1. Reception of the work order
  2. Preparation of the Field Intervention - for example: spare parts collection and site access authorization
  3. Execution of the Work Order
  4. Validation of alarm clearance with the Network Operation Center
  5. Reporting the outcome to the requestor; and the return of the spare parts (if applicable)

The most common metrics used to evaluate this function are:

  • Mean Time to Accept Work Order. Measures the average time taken by the Field Technician to accept a work order.
  • On-Time at Site Percentage. Percentage of times when the Field Technician arrives on time at the site compared to the total site visits.

3.1.2 Dispatch & Coordination Function

The Dispatch & Coordination function coordinates and dispatches work orders to Field Technicians, assuring that the Field Technician accepts the work order, is aware of the work order priority and begins to travel to the site. This function also handles site access authorization escalations to the Network Operation Center's Access Management function to guarantee Field Technicians access to the site.

This function includes the following tasks:

  1. Coordinating and dispatching work orders for corrective or preventive maintenance to the Field Services Technicians within the assigned geographical area
  2. Setting work order priorities according to the customer-defined SLA
  3. Interfacing with Access Management to confirm or escalate site access as needed
  4. Interface between the Field Technician and the Regional/Central Warehouse for spare parts
  5. Interfacing with the Field Technician for work order verification and closure

The metrics that are generally used to evaluate this function are:

  • Ticket volume. Number of tickets that are being handled over a specific pre-established period.
  • Mean Time to Dispatch Work Order. Measures the average time taken by the field coordinator to dispatch a work order to the Field Technician.
  • First Time Right. Percentage of tickets that were assigned to the correct field agent.

3.2 Field Maintenance Functions Selection & Customization.

The different options to customize the functions or teams that comprise the Field Force Management team will now be presented.

3.2.1 Field Coordinator/Dispatcher NOC Aggregation Setup

One of the most common aggregation of functions is performed by embedding the Field Coordinator/Dispatcher function into the Network Operation Center Tier 1 level functions. This translates into aggregating the Access Management and Field Dispatch functions into the NOC Tier 1 team.

This can prove effective when the NOC is monitoring a small network comprised of less than 250 sites (or if network monitoring is broken up into different regions, each one covered by a small team of engineers). In this scenario, the Tier 1 team will also be in charge of executing field dispatches (if necessary) and making the necessary adjustments to make sure that there will be access to the site for the Field Technicians.

As mentioned before, this option is best suited for region-based monitoring or for small networks, as the increase in functions makes them aware of all details for their respective sites, but in turn, the overhead associated with this setup may challenge the NOC Tier 1 team. The alternative configuration is to have a separate Field Manager or Field Dispatcher performing this function.

3.2.2 Field Force Leadership

Field Force Leadership duties include Field Force planning, controlling, steering, and reporting all aspects of Field Maintenance activities; management of Field Force organization and Field Force staffing; delegation and empowerment of Field Coordinators and team members. Within those duties, the following main activities are performed:

  1. Key Performance Indicators (KPI) measurement
  2. KPI and SLA reporting
  3. Shift management

Figure 3 shows the most common structure for Field Force Leadership, which will be explained in detail in the following paragraphs:

Figure 3. Field Force Leadership View.

As explained in Section 3.2.3 of the Network Operation Center module, each NOC group is usually supervised by a manager who delivers guidance to the entire team and serves as the Point of Contact (POC) for all those team's requirements or escalations. The Field Force main POC is typically the Field or Area manager (or the Dispatch & Coordination team in absence of the former) who is usually in charge of all the Field Force structure, including the Dispatch & Coordination function and the Field Technician teams (who undertake the Field Technician activities function). The entire team is supervised by a NOC/SOC Head, who acts as the ultimate responsible for the NOC, SOC and Field Maintenance operations.

Field Coordinators are regularly set as part of the structure. These people's expertise and process knowledge, supported by leadership empowerment, enables them to make controlled operative decisions during their shift in absence of the Field/Area manager. Field Coordinators usually respond to the acting Incident Manager on duty, who is the highest-ranking officer for any operative decision.

The NaaS operator may choose to leave out the Field/Area manager position in smaller networks, and thus the POC for field services would fall into the Field Coordinator's team or to the NOC Manager in absence of Field Coordinators as discussed in section 3.2.1.

The Field Maintenance team configuration is further discussed in Section 6, in which the model to be implemented and the team design and dimensioning is presented.


4 Field Maintenance Processes

In this section, the core processes followed by the Field Maintenance team are reviewed, including end-to-end process flows, key elements, roles and responsibilities and the most common metrics used to measure each process success.


4.1 Field Maintenance Processes Analysis

The following subsections present an analysis of the processes executed by the Field Maintenance team, including rationale, common KPIs and roles associated with each process.

4.1.1 Field Intervention

As explained in section 3.1.1, the activities performed by the field force include:

  1. Dispatch of the Work Order
  2. Preparation of the Field Intervention - for example: spare parts collection and site access authorization
  3. Execution of the Work Order
  4. Reporting the outcome to the requestor; and the return of the spare parts

Figure 4 presents the High level, end to end process flow for the Field Intervention process, which is also provided as a Template for customization by NaaS Operators.

'

Figure 4. Field Intervention High-Level Flowchart.

The High-level flow is closely reviewed below:

  1. The main input into this process is the creation of a Work Order. Work Order creation can be initiated by one of the following processes: Incident Management, Problem Management or Change Management. If a third party (such as a fiber company) requests access to the site, the work order is processed in the same manner as the field maintenance team would have to grant access to their own team, and would supervise the work performed on-site by the third party. Work order creation can be triggered manually, but also automatically via a field dispatch tool. The dispatcher assigns the Work order to the Field Technician.
  2. The Field Technician receives and accepts the Work Order.
  3. The Field Technician then starts preparing to work on the request. As part of this preparation, the dispatcher:
    1. Assures that access is granted, escalating to Access Management if needed
      The Field Technician in parallel:
    2. Requests for additional spares as required (which would trigger the Spare Parts Management process, covered in section 4.1.2)
    3. c. Verifies that access is granted to the site
  4. The Field Technician travels to the site after the preparation is done.
  5. Upon reaching the site the Field Technician checks for access, and for Health and Safety concerns, and books-on the site. The term 'book-on' is also known as a 'check-in' that is performed with the Network Operation Center to confirm the technician is on-site.
  6. At this point, the Field Technician checks with the NOC for any planned activity that may be scheduled. If that is the case, the information is raised to the dispatcher.
  7. If a planned activity is scheduled, the Field Technician checks network status with the Network Operation Center before executing the work order in order to prevent two different entities working on the site at the same time.
  8. If there is no planned activity in progress the Field Technician continues to execute the Work Order. During the execution of the task, the Field Technician:
    1. May ask for additional spares (which would trigger the Spare Parts Management process, covered in section 4.1.2)
    2. Request assistance from the Network Operation Center Tier 1 or Tier 2 level to complete the task
  9. After execution, the Field Technician verifies with the Network Operation Center if work has been successful. If not, the Field Technician may go back to the execution step.
  10. Once execution is verified successfully, the Field Technician reports the end of intervention and books-off the site. The term 'book-off' is also known as a 'check-out' that is performed with the Network Operation Center to confirm the technician has finished working and is ready to leave the site. After this, the work order is closed.
  11. 'Spare parts are returned, if applicable.

The most common KPI's utilized for Field Intervention are the following:

  • Mean Time of Work Order Duration. Calculates the average time for which a Work Order remains open.
  • On-Time Job Completion. Tracks the percentage of times when the Field Technicians complete the activity within the time defined in the SLA.

4.1.2 Spare Parts Management

Spare Parts Management is a particularly important process for the business, as the availability of a wide range of spare parts is a major factor for reduction in downtime when a hardware failure occurs. An efficient flow will guarantee that spare parts will be available for Field Technicians in the least amount of time to rectify a hardware failure occurring at the site at any given time.

This section focuses on the process performed by the Field Force organization, as other aspects regarding spare parts such as Stock Management are fully reviewed on the Logistics & Warehousing Module.

Figure 5 presents the High level, end to end process for the Spare Parts Management process, which is also provided as a Template for customization by NaaS Operators.

Figure 5. Spare Parts Management High-Level Flowchart.

The High-level flow is described as follows:

  1. The input for this process is the request for additional spares from a Field Technician. The request is triggered directly by the Field Technician with support from the Tier 2 level team in the Network Operation Center.
  2. The Warehouse receives the spare parts information request including model number and serial for each part requested.
  3. Warehouse personnel verifies the existence of the spares needed in the inventory, checking if the spare parts exist in the warehouse.
  4. If the spare parts exist, Warehouse updates the inventory accordingly for each part to be sent to the site.
  5. For every spare part that does not exist in the warehouse triggers a request to the vendor. This can be done by two different actions:
    1. The Warehouse executes a Return Material Authorization (RMA) to the vendor (if such type of contract exists with the vendor)
    2. The Warehouse introduces a part request to the corresponding team within the NaaS Organization so a Purchase Order is sent to the vendor to acquire the needed part
  6. The Field Technician obtains the part from the Warehouse or the vendor and travels to the site.
  7. Field Technician executes part replacement.

The most common KPI utilized for Spare Parts Management is the following:

  • Mean Time of Spare Delivery. Calculates the average time taken from the time the spare part request is received by the warehouse and the time it is delivered to the Field Technician.

4.1.3 Preventive Maintenance

Preventive Maintenance refers to scheduled, periodic maintenance of equipment to prevent unexpected breakdowns. This includes but is not restricted to tests, measurements and coordinated part replacements.

Preventive Maintenance is implemented through a schedule of planned maintenance with the aim of preventing breakdowns and failures. Preventive Maintenance can be a periodic activity, but it may also be a one-time activity. It can take the form of physical checks or logical activities like system backups. It ensures consumables such as equipment spares, are readily available should they be needed. Preventive Maintenance is not limited to physical checks, it should include regular system backups with offsite storage and checks for known errors as reported by the equipment vendors.

A Preventive Maintenance plan describes a list of activities to be performed on equipment according to a schedule - for example: daily, weekly, fortnightly, monthly, quarterly, and annually.

The primary goal of Preventive Maintenance is to avoid or mitigate equipment failures, and thereby reduce the cost that comes with corrective actions. This in turn will increase customer satisfaction. With Preventive Maintenance, it is ensured that all equipment is regularly checked and is ready for service when required.

Figure 6 presents the High level, end to end process for the Preventive Maintenance process, which is also provided as a Template for customization by NaaS Operators.

Figure 6. Preventive Maintenance High-Level Flowchart

The High-level flow for this process is detailed below:

  • The main input into this process is the reception of a preventive maintenance request. The Field Manager reviews the Preventive Maintenance Request.
  • The Field Manager creates or updates a Preventive Maintenance Plan.
  • The Field Manager engages with Operation and Maintenance for validation and approval of the Maintenance Plan. Typically, the Maintenance organization will validate and approve the plan and the Operations organization (represented by the Network Operation Center's NOC Manager or the Change Manager) will be consulted and advised of the corresponding plan.
  • Once O&M approves the plan, the Field Manager creates a Preventive Maintenance Ticket. At this point, it must be determined whether the activity is service impacting.
  • If the maintenance activity impacts service, then this should go through the Change Management process, which is fully reviewed in section 4.1.4 of the Network Operation Center module.
  • For non-service impacting maintenance activities, the Field Manager will assign the ticket and the activity to a Field Technician.
  • The Field Technician reviews the ticket and ensures that he understands the list of activities to be performed. In case of assistance from an external contractor is needed, the Field Technician guarantees such assistance will be provided on-site for the scheduled activity.
  • Before proceeding with the execution, the Field Technician will coordinate with the Network Operation Center team to ensure that the network is in good condition and that there are no conflicting activities, such as a change or incident.
  • Once the network condition is confirmed, the Field Technician will then proceed to execute the Preventive Maintenance activities.
  • 'After activity completion, the Field Technician creates a Preventive Maintenance Report, which is forwarded to the Field Manager.
  • The Field Technician then updates the ticket and closes the Preventive Maintenance ticket.

The most common KPI's utilized for Preventive Maintenance are the following: On-Time Preventive Maintenance and Preventive Maintenance Warranty.

  • On-Time Preventive Maintenance. Calculates the percentage of Preventive Maintenance activities completed within SLA.
  • Preventive Maintenance Warranty. Tracks that no fault occurs as a result of maintenance activities in any network element up to the following scheduled preventive maintenance visit.

4.2 Field Maintenance Process Selection & Configuration

Now that Field Maintenance processes have been analyzed, the NaaS Operator must select the processes to be implemented based on the Field Maintenance scope and the NaaS Organization capabilities and requirements.

Field Intervention is a mandatory process for the Field Force operation, as it is the main process followed by the Field Maintenance organization to troubleshoot and fix failures that are present on any site. Without such process in place, hardware failures would not be resolved, and service could be interrupted for the end-user, damaging the reputation of the NaaS operator.

There are two process adjustments that may be considered by the NaaS operator:

● Spare Parts Management

One option that may be considered by the NaaS operator is to remove the Warehouse from the Spare Parts process. Although feasible, this option is not recommended as this would translate into an absence of spare parts availability on a centralized, secure location. Alternatives are to have the Field Technicians stocking spare parts on their own which is not a secure option, or requesting parts directly with the vendor each time a spare part is needed, taking into consideration the time overhead this alternative implies.

● Preventive Maintenance

Another option that could be considered by the NaaS operator is eliminate the Preventive Maintenance process from the core processes executed by the field maintenance organization. This may become an alternative if the operator does not see fit to have a maintenance plan for the network, focusing on corrective interventions alone at least for the initial stages of the organization, or if the Field Technician team is comprised of a very few members and the operator does not want to have preventive maintenance activities occupying resources that could be focusing on corrective interventions.

5 Systems & Tools Overview

This section provides an examination of the systems and the most common tools used to support Field Maintenance, and aspects to consider for the NaaS operator evaluation and selection of tools to be incorporated into the field operations.


5.1 Tools High-Level View

There are three tools commonly used by a Field Maintenance organization to achieve its objectives. These are the Service Desk Management tool (which is also used by the Network Operation Center and the Service Operation Center organizations), a Dispatch Tool and a Network Inventory Management System (NIMS).

The Dispatch Tool can be regarded as the mandatory tool for the Field Maintenance team. While the SDM tool is a mandatory tool for the NOC and SOC, it can be viewed as an optional tool for the Field Maintenance team, as well as the NIMS tool. An overview of these tools is presented in the following sections.

5.1.1 Service Desk Management Tool

This tool is used for the documentation of all incidents, problems and changes that are being worked on the network at any given time. It can be considered one of the main tools used by the NOC and the SOC teams, as it enables the teams on both organizations to have a proper mechanism to document all activities surrounding each incident, problem or change ticket, including field interventions performed by the Field Maintenance Team.

A proper tool for documentation aids the team as they can know exactly what the status of any ticket is, what are the next items to be solved and what needs to be followed-up. It also enables reporting, which serves as the basis for Governance activities: revision of the Field Maintenance performance, KPIs, SLAs, etc.

A full Service Desk Management tool is a very powerful tool that incorporates modules for Incident Management (Incident tickets), Problem Management (Problem tickets) and Change Management (Change tickets). It also includes a Knowledge Base module (KB Articles) that serves as a repository for all the KB information used by all NOC, SOC and Field Maintenance teams, updated with key past experiences and lessons learned for future reference and improvements.

As discussed in section 5.1.1 of the Network Operation Center Module, another alternative to consider is to have a Ticketing tool instead of an SDM tool, if the focus will be set on fault restoral and less on Problem or Change Management. This option can be taken by the operator regardless of having a NOC/SOC/Field Maintenance structure in place as all three organizations could benefit from the SDM tool but other alternatives are available as well. This will be further discussed in section 5.2.

5.1.2 Dispatch Tool

This tool aids the Field Maintenance organization to manage the dispatch and life cycle of work orders generated to the Field Technicians, streamlining work requests management and reporting, which in turn, improves efficiency and productivity for the field maintenance team. This tool enables the dispatcher/coordinator function to ensure the right deployment for the right Field Technician is executed, and helps on planning, scheduling and tracking activities for the field force team.

The dispatch tool includes features such as job scheduling, route and location tracking, and usually includes dashboards showing work order status and SLA status for active and historical work requests handled by the field maintenance team. 5.1.3 Network Inventory Management System

The Network Inventory Management System (usually known as NIMS) is a tool that aids the Field Maintenance organization to manage all the assets on the network. This enables the field area manager or the field coordinator/dispatcher to have records of all the equipment that is installed within the network, and have up-to-date the information accordingly with the changes or replacements that are executed by the Field Maintenance team.

The NIMS tool allows for a precise view of inventory status at any given time and ensures better control of the network assets. It is a useful tool that aids the field maintenance management team to know when to acquire new assets/spare parts or to decommission old ones, which can help with general network performance and uptime, along with capacity planning. This tool helps to keep track of equipment in a more efficient manner.

Examples of commercial tools are Ericsson Adaptive Inventory, Amdocs Cramer, Nokia Inventory Data Manager or Oracle Unified Inventory Management. 5.2 Systems Selection

In the previous section tools that are part of the Field Maintenance solution were introduced. Now aspects to consider for tool evaluation and selection will be presented. First, the critical operational tools will be discussed, followed by optional support tools.

5.2.1 Critical Operational Tools

There is a tool that can be considered as critical and needs to be part of the Field Maintenance solution incorporated by the NaaS operator:

  • Dispatch Tool
    A Dispatch tool can be of great aid for the NOC and Field Maintenance teams, as the process to dispatch Field Technicians to sites can become transparent to both organizations with such a tool in place.
    Most of the dispatch tools are configurable so they can be easily integrated into the Service Desk Management tool and dispatch to sites can be executed with ease by the Network Operation Center by just a few clicks.
    The NaaS operator can benefit from the use of this tool as the NOC and the Field Maintenance entities can become more agile and management tasks such as reporting or governance are made easier with the incorporation of this tool.
    Since the full benefit of dispatch automation can be achieved by the integration of the SDM tool and a Dispatch tool, which may be too expensive for a starting or a small organization, the NaaS operator may choose to leave this tool out of their solution, and use a very simple alternative to dispatch Field Technicians to sites, which is relying on the telephone system. After the NOC has determined that assistance from a field tech is needed, they can note their ticket and call the actual Field Technician directly to let them know that they need to dispatch to a site.
    This of course leaves out most of the benefits this tool can bring to the organization, such as:
    1. Automatic assignment of field crews
    2. Dispatch timestamps for each stage of the work order
    3. Route and location tracking
    4. Reporting

    Since all transactions would be executed manually and followed-up by phone calls between the agents involved in the work order (Field Technician, dispatcher/coordinator and the NOC), SLA/KPI analysis, which are part of the management tasks become more difficult as most of the tracking is performed manually, which requires review of every ticket's note to get the information that is needed for reporting.

5.2.2 Support Tools

In the following paragraphs, the Service Desk Management and the Network Inventory Management System tools will be discussed, which are support tools that are optional depending on the needs of the NaaS operator.

● Service Desk Management Tool

A full Service Desk Management tool can be of great aid for the NOC and SOC teams (covered in both Network Operation Center module and Service Operation Center module), but the need for such a tool is very limited for the Field Maintenance team. If the Dispatch tool is in place in the organization, another benefit that usually spans from its use is that updates executed by the Field Technician are automatically synced in the ticket directly (whereas it's an incident, change or problem ticket).

If such synchronization is not in place or if the dispatch tool is not acquired by the organization, the most common practice is to have the Network Operation Center team updating the ticket with relevant notes spanning from the interactions with the field maintenance team (usually via phone calls).

The NaaS operator can consider that there are free SDM tools available in the market, and some of them have open trials. By taking advantage of this, the Field Maintenance team could explore the tool to assess if its use is beneficial for their organization.

● Network Inventory Management System Tool

A Network Inventory Management System can bring value to an organization that handles a highly distributed spare part system or for an organization that handles several levels of warehouses (local, regional, centralized). It can also be beneficial for a NaaS operator that will keep in control not only the physical assets of the network, but also the software and configurations that are present for each element in the network.

If the NaaS operator considers that the cost associated is too high for the benefits it may bring, the operator could implement a much simpler network inventory system using available tools like Google Docs, Office 365 or Dropbox, keeping records and control of all spare parts using their associated spreadsheet tools This will make reporting for management more difficult, but is one of the most practical alternatives to keep control of the equipment that is physically present on a warehouse, and can also be used to keep control of the parts that are installed on the sites.

6.1 Staffing Model Assessment

There are two main models available for Field Maintenance staffing (which apply to NOC and SOC staffing as well) that can be considered by the NaaS operator: In-sourcing (building Field Maintenance team with internal resources as part of the NaaS Organization) and Out-sourcing (reaching out to a third party to take control of field operations). An optional third alternative is also available, in which the NaaS operator may choose to outsource some tasks (such as the Field Intervention function) while retaining the other part of the operation (such as the leadership function, referred to as the Dispatcher & Coordination function).

Please refer to section 6.1 of the Network Operation Center module for a complete discussion on the Staffing Model Assessment, as the three models discussed there (Insourced, Outsourced and Mixed Schema) apply as well for a Field Maintenance staffing strategy.


6.2 Service levels Definition

Service levels are determined by the severity of the incident that has been created, which are assigned by the Network Operation Center organization. As such, the first thing that needs to be defined by the NaaS operator is the criticality matrix. Please refer to the Network Operation Center module for a complete discussion on the NOC's service level settings.

After the criticality matrix is set, the NaaS Operator must set two variables for each category:

  • Response Time. The time in which the NOC must respond with a trouble ticket to any alarm or report occurred
  • Restoration Time. The time in which all services/faults impacted must be restored

The first variable is strictly Field Maintenance dependent and is usually expressed in minutes (X minutes from dispatch creation to dispatch acknowledge and field tech assignment).

The second variable needs consideration of several factors, including average travel time, spare part pick-up, and the agreed contractual SLAs for new parts that may be required and that are not existent in the warehouse.

It is important to note that the SLA for the Field Maintenance Team will be closely related to the NOC SLA and NOC Response + Analysis time. For example, if the NOC SLA is 8 hours, the NOC response time is 15 minutes, and the NOC analysis time is 30 minutes, then the Field Maintenance SLA will be 7 hours 45 minutes.

2 Field Maintenance Fundamentals

This section provides a general overview of the Field Maintenance team, including the organization structure and the scope for each team highlighting the relevance and potential impact for NaaS Operators.


2.1 Field Maintenance Overview

Before diving into the overview of the Field Maintenance team, the term Maintenance will be reviewed. Maintenance is often referred to as the actions executed with the objective of retaining or restoring equipment to a state in which it can perform its required function. These activities ensure that production systems and equipment operate consistently in a steady state of defined functionality and performance.

The Field Force team, also known as Field Maintenance or First Line Maintenance, is the team responsible for all on-site activities on the network. Field Technicians are the eyes, ears, and hands of the Network Operation Center at any site, performing activities like:

  1. Assisting troubleshooting on-site
  2. Fixing hardware-related events
  3. Connecting power plants or generators in case of commercial outage scenarios
  4. Validating site conditions
  5. Granting access to equipment to external service providers such as fiber company technicians.

The Field Force team is in charge of executing both corrective and preventive field interventions. Corrective interventions are the set of tasks performed to identify, isolate, and rectify faults on a failed equipment, which are commonly sent to Field Technicians via incident tickets created by the Network Operation Center organization. Preventive interventions are a set of tasks performed with a certain pre-defined periodicity (also referred to as scheduled or planned maintenance) to maintain equipment in satisfactory operating conditions, detecting and correcting incipient failures before they occur.

Field Maintenance is a crucial team on a network, as they serve as the vehicle to restore any on-site failure (corrective interventions) and to ensure optimum performance and prevent problems before they arise (preventive interventions). They are also in charge of spare parts handling and replacements performed at the site, when applicable. The NaaS operator must consider this team as a critical factor for their operation, since an efficient field workforce will enable increased business productivity and improved reputation by decreasing the turnaround time for repair services.


2.2 Field Maintenance Organization

From a functional view, the Field Maintenance team includes the following functions:

  1. Field Technician Activities
  2. b) Dispatcher & Coordination Function

Both functions will be covered in depth in Section 3 of this module, where guidance will be provided for the NaaS Operator to define the structure that best matches the intended scope of the organization.

From a process view, the Field Maintenance team includes the following responsibilities:

  1. Field Intervention
  2. Spare Parts Management
  3. Preventive Maintenance

These processes will be covered in depth in Section 4 of this module. The NaaS operator will be presented with alternatives to customize the processes according to its requirements and capabilities.

The teams working within the Field Maintenance organization coordinate their efforts with the Network Operation Center in order to prevent or minimize any unplanned interruption on the network or a reduction/degradation in the quality of the service delivered to the end-user. While the end-to-end flow will be presented in Section 4, a high-level overview of the interactions of the teams is presented below:

  1. Network surveillance is recorded by the Network Operation Centers Fault Management Tier 1 team and escalated to the Field Maintenance team if on-site assistance is required
  2. Field Coordinator/Dispatcher assures the ticket or work order is accepted by the Field Technician, and supervises travel time and distance
  3. Access Management is invoked when field assistance is needed and access to premises is required
  4. Field Technician performs activities on-site to rectify the fault and validates results back with the Network Operation Center
  5. Preventive maintenance tasks are planned, agreed, scheduled and executed between the Network Operation Center organization and the Filed Force organization.

3 Field Maintenance Functions

This section presents the definition of each function executed by the Field Maintenance team, focusing on its vital aspects and the configuration possibilities for the NaaS Operator.


3.1 Field Maintenance Functions Overview

The subsections below provide a drill-down into the functions of the Field Maintenance team, analyzing the key success factors and relevance/need for each function:

3.1.1 Field Technician Activities

The Field Technician activities function is the most vital function that is supported by the Field Force organization. Field Techs take over site assistance on sites, commonly for 24 hours a day and 365 days a year (although other arrangements do exist, as 8x5, 16x5, etc.), performing tasks to resolve faults on-site or assist with on-hands support in troubleshooting efforts along with the Network Operation Center's Tier 1 or Tier 2 engineers. The most common setup is to have a Field Technician crew consisting of at least two field techs working on a site at any given time; this is to facilitate work at height and for health & safety concerns in remote areas. However, the NaaS Operator may consider to have only one technician on site for simple preventive maintenance tasks that do not require work at height. From this point forward, every time a Field Technician is mentioned on this document it is translated to a Field Technician crew.

Field Technician function typically includes the following activities:

  1. Reception of the work order
  2. Preparation of the Field Intervention - for example: spare parts collection and site access authorization
  3. Execution of the Work Order
  4. Validation of alarm clearance with the Network Operation Center
  5. Reporting the outcome to the requestor; and the return of the spare parts (if applicable)

The most common metrics used to evaluate this function are:

  • Mean Time to Accept Work Order. Measures the average time taken by the Field Technician to accept a work order.
  • On-Time at Site Percentage. Percentage of times when the Field Technician arrives on time at the site compared to the total site visits.

3.1.2 Dispatch & Coordination Function

The Dispatch & Coordination function coordinates and dispatches work orders to Field Technicians, assuring that the Field Technician accepts the work order, is aware of the work order priority and begins to travel to the site. This function also handles site access authorization escalations to the Network Operation Center's Access Management function to guarantee Field Technicians access to the site.

This function includes the following tasks:

  1. Coordinating and dispatching work orders for corrective or preventive maintenance to the Field Services Technicians within the assigned geographical area
  2. Setting work order priorities according to the customer-defined SLA
  3. Interfacing with Access Management to confirm or escalate site access as needed
  4. Interface between the Field Technician and the Regional/Central Warehouse for spare parts
  5. Interfacing with the Field Technician for work order verification and closure

The metrics that are generally used to evaluate this function are:

  • Ticket volume. Number of tickets that are being handled over a specific pre-established period.
  • Mean Time to Dispatch Work Order. Measures the average time taken by the field coordinator to dispatch a work order to the Field Technician.
  • First Time Right. Percentage of tickets that were assigned to the correct field agent.

3.2 Field Maintenance Functions Selection & Customization.

The different options to customize the functions or teams that comprise the Field Force Management team will now be presented.

3.2.1 Field Coordinator/Dispatcher NOC Aggregation Setup

One of the most common aggregation of functions is performed by embedding the Field Coordinator/Dispatcher function into the Network Operation Center Tier 1 level functions. This translates into aggregating the Access Management and Field Dispatch functions into the NOC Tier 1 team.

This can prove effective when the NOC is monitoring a small network comprised of less than 250 sites (or if network monitoring is broken up into different regions, each one covered by a small team of engineers). In this scenario, the Tier 1 team will also be in charge of executing field dispatches (if necessary) and making the necessary adjustments to make sure that there will be access to the site for the Field Technicians.

As mentioned before, this option is best suited for region-based monitoring or for small networks, as the increase in functions makes them aware of all details for their respective sites, but in turn, the overhead associated with this setup may challenge the NOC Tier 1 team. The alternative configuration is to have a separate Field Manager or Field Dispatcher performing this function.

3.2.2 Field Force Leadership

Field Force Leadership duties include Field Force planning, controlling, steering, and reporting all aspects of Field Maintenance activities; management of Field Force organization and Field Force staffing; delegation and empowerment of Field Coordinators and team members. Within those duties, the following main activities are performed:

  1. Key Performance Indicators (KPI) measurement
  2. KPI and SLA reporting
  3. Shift management

Figure 3 shows the most common structure for Field Force Leadership, which will be explained in detail in the following paragraphs:

Figure 3. Field Force Leadership View.

As explained in Section 3.2.3 of the Network Operation Center module, each NOC group is usually supervised by a manager who delivers guidance to the entire team and serves as the Point of Contact (POC) for all those team's requirements or escalations. The Field Force main POC is typically the Field or Area manager (or the Dispatch & Coordination team in absence of the former) who is usually in charge of all the Field Force structure, including the Dispatch & Coordination function and the Field Technician teams (who undertake the Field Technician activities function). The entire team is supervised by a NOC/SOC Head, who acts as the ultimate responsible for the NOC, SOC and Field Maintenance operations.

Field Coordinators are regularly set as part of the structure. These people's expertise and process knowledge, supported by leadership empowerment, enables them to make controlled operative decisions during their shift in absence of the Field/Area manager. Field Coordinators usually respond to the acting Incident Manager on duty, who is the highest-ranking officer for any operative decision.

The NaaS operator may choose to leave out the Field/Area manager position in smaller networks, and thus the POC for field services would fall into the Field Coordinator's team or to the NOC Manager in absence of Field Coordinators as discussed in section 3.2.1.

The Field Maintenance team configuration is further discussed in Section 6, in which the model to be implemented and the team design and dimensioning is presented.


4 Field Maintenance Processes

In this section, the core processes followed by the Field Maintenance team are reviewed, including end-to-end process flows, key elements, roles and responsibilities and the most common metrics used to measure each process success.


4.1 Field Maintenance Processes Analysis

The following subsections present an analysis of the processes executed by the Field Maintenance team, including rationale, common KPIs and roles associated with each process.

4.1.1 Field Intervention

As explained in section 3.1.1, the activities performed by the field force include:

  1. Dispatch of the Work Order
  2. Preparation of the Field Intervention - for example: spare parts collection and site access authorization
  3. Execution of the Work Order
  4. Reporting the outcome to the requestor; and the return of the spare parts

Figure 4 presents the High level, end to end process flow for the Field Intervention process, which is also provided as a Template for customization by NaaS Operators.

'

Figure 4. Field Intervention High-Level Flowchart.

The High-level flow is closely reviewed below:

  1. The main input into this process is the creation of a Work Order. Work Order creation can be initiated by one of the following processes: Incident Management, Problem Management or Change Management. If a third party (such as a fiber company) requests access to the site, the work order is processed in the same manner as the field maintenance team would have to grant access to their own team, and would supervise the work performed on-site by the third party. Work order creation can be triggered manually, but also automatically via a field dispatch tool. The dispatcher assigns the Work order to the Field Technician.
  2. The Field Technician receives and accepts the Work Order.
  3. The Field Technician then starts preparing to work on the request. As part of this preparation, the dispatcher:
    1. Assures that access is granted, escalating to Access Management if needed
      The Field Technician in parallel:
    2. Requests for additional spares as required (which would trigger the Spare Parts Management process, covered in section 4.1.2)
    3. c. Verifies that access is granted to the site
  4. The Field Technician travels to the site after the preparation is done.
  5. Upon reaching the site the Field Technician checks for access, and for Health and Safety concerns, and books-on the site. The term 'book-on' is also known as a 'check-in' that is performed with the Network Operation Center to confirm the technician is on-site.
  6. At this point, the Field Technician checks with the NOC for any planned activity that may be scheduled. If that is the case, the information is raised to the dispatcher.
  7. If a planned activity is scheduled, the Field Technician checks network status with the Network Operation Center before executing the work order in order to prevent two different entities working on the site at the same time.
  8. If there is no planned activity in progress the Field Technician continues to execute the Work Order. During the execution of the task, the Field Technician:
    1. May ask for additional spares (which would trigger the Spare Parts Management process, covered in section 4.1.2)
    2. Request assistance from the Network Operation Center Tier 1 or Tier 2 level to complete the task
  9. After execution, the Field Technician verifies with the Network Operation Center if work has been successful. If not, the Field Technician may go back to the execution step.
  10. Once execution is verified successfully, the Field Technician reports the end of intervention and books-off the site. The term 'book-off' is also known as a 'check-out' that is performed with the Network Operation Center to confirm the technician has finished working and is ready to leave the site. After this, the work order is closed.
  11. 'Spare parts are returned, if applicable.

The most common KPI's utilized for Field Intervention are the following:

  • Mean Time of Work Order Duration. Calculates the average time for which a Work Order remains open.
  • On-Time Job Completion. Tracks the percentage of times when the Field Technicians complete the activity within the time defined in the SLA.

4.1.2 Spare Parts Management

Spare Parts Management is a particularly important process for the business, as the availability of a wide range of spare parts is a major factor for reduction in downtime when a hardware failure occurs. An efficient flow will guarantee that spare parts will be available for Field Technicians in the least amount of time to rectify a hardware failure occurring at the site at any given time.

This section focuses on the process performed by the Field Force organization, as other aspects regarding spare parts such as Stock Management are fully reviewed on the Logistics & Warehousing Module.

Figure 5 presents the High level, end to end process for the Spare Parts Management process, which is also provided as a Template for customization by NaaS Operators.

Figure 5. Spare Parts Management High-Level Flowchart.

The High-level flow is described as follows:

  1. The input for this process is the request for additional spares from a Field Technician. The request is triggered directly by the Field Technician with support from the Tier 2 level team in the Network Operation Center.
  2. The Warehouse receives the spare parts information request including model number and serial for each part requested.
  3. Warehouse personnel verifies the existence of the spares needed in the inventory, checking if the spare parts exist in the warehouse.
  4. If the spare parts exist, Warehouse updates the inventory accordingly for each part to be sent to the site.
  5. For every spare part that does not exist in the warehouse triggers a request to the vendor. This can be done by two different actions:
    1. The Warehouse executes a Return Material Authorization (RMA) to the vendor (if such type of contract exists with the vendor)
    2. The Warehouse introduces a part request to the corresponding team within the NaaS Organization so a Purchase Order is sent to the vendor to acquire the needed part
  6. The Field Technician obtains the part from the Warehouse or the vendor and travels to the site.
  7. Field Technician executes part replacement.

The most common KPI utilized for Spare Parts Management is the following:

  • Mean Time of Spare Delivery. Calculates the average time taken from the time the spare part request is received by the warehouse and the time it is delivered to the Field Technician.

4.1.3 Preventive Maintenance

Preventive Maintenance refers to scheduled, periodic maintenance of equipment to prevent unexpected breakdowns. This includes but is not restricted to tests, measurements and coordinated part replacements.

Preventive Maintenance is implemented through a schedule of planned maintenance with the aim of preventing breakdowns and failures. Preventive Maintenance can be a periodic activity, but it may also be a one-time activity. It can take the form of physical checks or logical activities like system backups. It ensures consumables such as equipment spares, are readily available should they be needed. Preventive Maintenance is not limited to physical checks, it should include regular system backups with offsite storage and checks for known errors as reported by the equipment vendors.

A Preventive Maintenance plan describes a list of activities to be performed on equipment according to a schedule - for example: daily, weekly, fortnightly, monthly, quarterly, and annually.

The primary goal of Preventive Maintenance is to avoid or mitigate equipment failures, and thereby reduce the cost that comes with corrective actions. This in turn will increase customer satisfaction. With Preventive Maintenance, it is ensured that all equipment is regularly checked and is ready for service when required.

Figure 6 presents the High level, end to end process for the Preventive Maintenance process, which is also provided as a Template for customization by NaaS Operators.

Figure 6. Preventive Maintenance High-Level Flowchart

The High-level flow for this process is detailed below:

  • The main input into this process is the reception of a preventive maintenance request. The Field Manager reviews the Preventive Maintenance Request.
  • The Field Manager creates or updates a Preventive Maintenance Plan.
  • The Field Manager engages with Operation and Maintenance for validation and approval of the Maintenance Plan. Typically, the Maintenance organization will validate and approve the plan and the Operations organization (represented by the Network Operation Center's NOC Manager or the Change Manager) will be consulted and advised of the corresponding plan.
  • Once O&M approves the plan, the Field Manager creates a Preventive Maintenance Ticket. At this point, it must be determined whether the activity is service impacting.
  • If the maintenance activity impacts service, then this should go through the Change Management process, which is fully reviewed in section 4.1.4 of the Network Operation Center module.
  • For non-service impacting maintenance activities, the Field Manager will assign the ticket and the activity to a Field Technician.
  • The Field Technician reviews the ticket and ensures that he understands the list of activities to be performed. In case of assistance from an external contractor is needed, the Field Technician guarantees such assistance will be provided on-site for the scheduled activity.
  • Before proceeding with the execution, the Field Technician will coordinate with the Network Operation Center team to ensure that the network is in good condition and that there are no conflicting activities, such as a change or incident.
  • Once the network condition is confirmed, the Field Technician will then proceed to execute the Preventive Maintenance activities.
  • 'After activity completion, the Field Technician creates a Preventive Maintenance Report, which is forwarded to the Field Manager.
  • The Field Technician then updates the ticket and closes the Preventive Maintenance ticket.

The most common KPI's utilized for Preventive Maintenance are the following: On-Time Preventive Maintenance and Preventive Maintenance Warranty.

  • On-Time Preventive Maintenance. Calculates the percentage of Preventive Maintenance activities completed within SLA.
  • Preventive Maintenance Warranty. Tracks that no fault occurs as a result of maintenance activities in any network element up to the following scheduled preventive maintenance visit.

4.2 Field Maintenance Process Selection & Configuration

Now that Field Maintenance processes have been analyzed, the NaaS Operator must select the processes to be implemented based on the Field Maintenance scope and the NaaS Organization capabilities and requirements.

Field Intervention is a mandatory process for the Field Force operation, as it is the main process followed by the Field Maintenance organization to troubleshoot and fix failures that are present on any site. Without such process in place, hardware failures would not be resolved, and service could be interrupted for the end-user, damaging the reputation of the NaaS operator.

There are two process adjustments that may be considered by the NaaS operator:

● Spare Parts Management

One option that may be considered by the NaaS operator is to remove the Warehouse from the Spare Parts process. Although feasible, this option is not recommended as this would translate into an absence of spare parts availability on a centralized, secure location. Alternatives are to have the Field Technicians stocking spare parts on their own which is not a secure option, or requesting parts directly with the vendor each time a spare part is needed, taking into consideration the time overhead this alternative implies.

● Preventive Maintenance

Another option that could be considered by the NaaS operator is eliminate the Preventive Maintenance process from the core processes executed by the field maintenance organization. This may become an alternative if the operator does not see fit to have a maintenance plan for the network, focusing on corrective interventions alone at least for the initial stages of the organization, or if the Field Technician team is comprised of a very few members and the operator does not want to have preventive maintenance activities occupying resources that could be focusing on corrective interventions.

5 Systems & Tools Overview

This section provides an examination of the systems and the most common tools used to support Field Maintenance, and aspects to consider for the NaaS operator evaluation and selection of tools to be incorporated into the field operations.


5.1 Tools High-Level View

There are three tools commonly used by a Field Maintenance organization to achieve its objectives. These are the Service Desk Management tool (which is also used by the Network Operation Center and the Service Operation Center organizations), a Dispatch Tool and a Network Inventory Management System (NIMS).

The Dispatch Tool can be regarded as the mandatory tool for the Field Maintenance team. While the SDM tool is a mandatory tool for the NOC and SOC, it can be viewed as an optional tool for the Field Maintenance team, as well as the NIMS tool. An overview of these tools is presented in the following sections.

5.1.1 Service Desk Management Tool

This tool is used for the documentation of all incidents, problems and changes that are being worked on the network at any given time. It can be considered one of the main tools used by the NOC and the SOC teams, as it enables the teams on both organizations to have a proper mechanism to document all activities surrounding each incident, problem or change ticket, including field interventions performed by the Field Maintenance Team.

A proper tool for documentation aids the team as they can know exactly what the status of any ticket is, what are the next items to be solved and what needs to be followed-up. It also enables reporting, which serves as the basis for Governance activities: revision of the Field Maintenance performance, KPIs, SLAs, etc.

A full Service Desk Management tool is a very powerful tool that incorporates modules for Incident Management (Incident tickets), Problem Management (Problem tickets) and Change Management (Change tickets). It also includes a Knowledge Base module (KB Articles) that serves as a repository for all the KB information used by all NOC, SOC and Field Maintenance teams, updated with key past experiences and lessons learned for future reference and improvements.

As discussed in section 5.1.1 of the Network Operation Center Module, another alternative to consider is to have a Ticketing tool instead of an SDM tool, if the focus will be set on fault restoral and less on Problem or Change Management. This option can be taken by the operator regardless of having a NOC/SOC/Field Maintenance structure in place as all three organizations could benefit from the SDM tool but other alternatives are available as well. This will be further discussed in section 5.2.

5.1.2 Dispatch Tool

This tool aids the Field Maintenance organization to manage the dispatch and life cycle of work orders generated to the Field Technicians, streamlining work requests management and reporting, which in turn, improves efficiency and productivity for the field maintenance team. 5.1.3 Network Inventory Management System

The Network Inventory Management System (usually known as NIMS) is a tool that aids the Field Maintenance organization to manage all the assets on the network. 5.2 Systems Selection

In the previous section tools that are part of the Field Maintenance solution were introduced. Now aspects to consider for tool evaluation and selection will be presented. First, the critical operational tools will be discussed, followed by optional support tools.

5.2.1 Critical Operational Tools

There is a tool that can be considered as critical and needs to be part of the Field Maintenance solution incorporated by the NaaS operator:

  • Dispatch Tool
    A Dispatch tool can be of great aid for the NOC and Field Maintenance teams, as the process to dispatch Field Technicians to sites can become transparent to both organizations with such a tool in place.
    Most of the dispatch tools are configurable so they can be easily integrated into the Service Desk Management tool and dispatch to sites can be executed with ease by the Network Operation Center by just a few clicks.
    The NaaS operator can benefit from the use of this tool as the NOC and the Field Maintenance entities can become more agile and management tasks such as reporting or governance are made easier with the incorporation of this tool.
    Since the full benefit of dispatch automation can be achieved by the integration of the SDM tool and a Dispatch tool, which may be too expensive for a starting or a small organization, the NaaS operator may choose to leave this tool out of their solution, and use a very simple alternative to dispatch Field Technicians to sites, which is relying on the telephone system. After the NOC has determined that assistance from a field tech is needed, they can note their ticket and call the actual Field Technician directly to let them know that they need to dispatch to a site.
    This of course leaves out most of the benefits this tool can bring to the organization, such as:
    1. Automatic assignment of field crews
    2. Dispatch timestamps for each stage of the work order
    3. Route and location tracking
    4. Reporting

    Since all transactions would be executed manually and followed-up by phone calls between the agents involved in the work order (Field Technician, dispatcher/coordinator and the NOC), SLA/KPI analysis, which are part of the management tasks become more difficult as most of the tracking is performed manually, which requires review of every ticket's note to get the information that is needed for reporting.

5.2.2 Support Tools

In the following paragraphs, the Service Desk Management and the Network Inventory Management System tools will be discussed, which are support tools that are optional depending on the needs of the NaaS operator.

● Service Desk Management Tool

A full Service Desk Management tool can be of great aid for the NOC and SOC teams (covered in both Network Operation Center module and Service Operation Center module), but the need for such a tool is very limited for the Field Maintenance team. If the Dispatch tool is in place in the organization, another benefit that usually spans from its use is that updates executed by the Field Technician are automatically synced in the ticket directly (whereas it's an incident, change or problem ticket).

If such synchronization is not in place or if the dispatch tool is not acquired by the organization, the most common practice is to have the Network Operation Center team updating the ticket with relevant notes spanning from the interactions with the field maintenance team (usually via phone calls).

The NaaS operator can consider that there are free SDM tools available in the market, and some of them have open trials. By taking advantage of this, the Field Maintenance team could explore the tool to assess if its use is beneficial for their organization.

● Network Inventory Management System Tool

A Network Inventory Management System can bring value to an organization that handles a highly distributed spare part system or for an organization that handles several levels of warehouses (local, regional, centralized). It can also be beneficial for a NaaS operator that will keep in control not only the physical assets of the network, but also the software and configurations that are present for each element in the network.

If the NaaS operator considers that the cost associated is too high for the benefits it may bring, the operator could implement a much simpler network inventory system using available tools like Google Docs, Office 365 or Dropbox, keeping records and control of all spare parts using their associated spreadsheet tools This will make reporting for management more difficult, but is one of the most practical alternatives to keep control of the equipment that is physically present on a warehouse, and can also be used to keep control of the parts that are installed on the sites.

6 Field Maintenance Implementation

This section presents a discussion of the available implementation options and directives for NaaS Operators to define their own staffing model, service levels and dimensioning of the Field Maintenance staff.


6.1 Staffing Model Assessment

There are two main models available for Field Maintenance staffing (which apply to NOC and SOC staffing as well) that can be considered by the NaaS operator: In-sourcing (building Field Maintenance team with internal resources as part of the NaaS Organization) and Out-sourcing (reaching out to a third party to take control of field operations). An optional third alternative is also available, in which the NaaS operator may choose to outsource some tasks (such as the Field Intervention function) while retaining the other part of the operation (such as the leadership function, referred to as the Dispatcher & Coordination function).

Please refer to section 6.1 of the Network Operation Center module for a complete discussion on the Staffing Model Assessment, as the three models discussed there (Insourced, Outsourced and Mixed Schema) apply as well for a Field Maintenance staffing strategy.


6.2 Service levels Definition

Service levels are determined by the severity of the incident that has been created, which are assigned by the Network Operation Center organization. As such, the first thing that needs to be defined by the NaaS operator is the criticality matrix. Please refer to the Network Operation Center module for a complete discussion on the NOC's service level settings.

After the criticality matrix is set, the NaaS Operator must set two variables for each category:

  • Response Time. The time in which the NOC must respond with a trouble ticket to any alarm or report occurred
  • Restoration Time. The time in which all services/faults impacted must be restored

The first variable is strictly Field Maintenance dependent and is usually expressed in minutes (X minutes from dispatch creation to dispatch acknowledge and field tech assignment).

The second variable needs consideration of several factors, including average travel time, spare part pick-up, and the agreed contractual SLAs for new parts that may be required and that are not existent in the warehouse.

It is important to note that the SLA for the Field Maintenance Team will be closely related to the NOC SLA and NOC Response + Analysis time. For example, if the NOC SLA is 8 hours, the NOC response time is 15 minutes, and the NOC analysis time is 30 minutes, then the Field Maintenance SLA will be 7 hours 45 minutes.
