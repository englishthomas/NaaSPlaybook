1 Introduction

The network operation center (NOC) is a functional team that specializes in monitoring, troubleshooting and responding to undesired events on a network which have a direct or potential impact on the networks availability and performance, or affecting end users. NOC services and responsibilities include, amongst others:

  • Registering all alarms and incidents as trouble tickets (TT) and assignment of each TT to the appropriate area or service responsible for their timely resolution.
  • Undertaking appropriate actions in relation to any alarm from the network in order to identify, diagnose, isolate, contain, stop and resolve the event causing the alarm.
  • Managing information regarding the execution of any activity that will or could impact/affect the network.
  • Reporting the current network status to relevant stakeholders at any time.

This module presents an introduction to the functions, processes and tools that operate within a NOC, and establishes guidelines for the NaaS operators to define the structure of their own NOC, customize the processes that will be implemented, evaluate and select the tools that will support the operation, and define the implementation model to be followed along with the dimensioning of the NOC team and facilities.


1.1 Module Objectives

This module will immerse the NaaS operator into the functions and processes of the NOC and will provide the necessary directions to define processes and resources and identify required systems for the NaaS operators NOC. The specific objectives of this module are to:

  1. Provide a generic overview of a network operation center, providing guidance to the NaaS Operator to define the NOC basic structure according to their requirements and capabilities.
  2. Enable the NaaS operator to customize the core processes to be implemented at the NOC, including roles and responsibilities, and the corresponding process flows.
  3. Provide a high-level view of the tools that support the processes of a NOC, enabling the NaaS operator to identify the required tools to be part of their solution.
  4. Provide guidelines to assess the NOC implementation model, including the dimensioning of the team and determination of the NOC facility requirements.

2 NOC Fundamentals

This section provides an overview of the NOC, including the organization structure and the scope for each team highlighting the relevance and potential impact for NaaS operators.


2.1 NOC Overview

The NOC is the entity responsible for monitoring and responding to any alarm or unwanted condition that may have an impact on the network, potentially affecting end customers. The NOC can be considered the first line of defense against any disruption on the network. The NOC enables centralized supervision, control and maintenance of all equipment and network domain elements on a NaaS operator network.

A NOC responds to deviations from a normal network status like:

  • Electrical events — such as power outages
  • Transmission events — such as backhaul, fronthaul or backbone failures
  • Hardware events — such as component failures
  • Software events — such as configuration changes
  • External events — such as DOS attacks

The NOC will analyze the situation, perform troubleshooting, engage necessary fix agents, and follow up until the network has restored to the previous, normal condition.

The main responsibilities of the NOC may include:

  • Monitoring of all critical network elements
  • Identification of failures and network degradations with both a reactive and proactive approach
  • Determination of network availability and performance
  • Event and incident management including escalations to expedite failure restoral
  • Change and configuration management
  • Documentation of all failures and performance issues
  • Reporting on possible optimization scenarios based on trends

The main responsibility of the NOC is to react as quickly as possible to any problem that may arise on the network, getting out in front of issues and fixing them before they impact their end customers or other MNO end customers. The benefits that a NOC organization brings to NaaS operators are:

  • A NOC responds efficiently to network deviations on a 24x7 basis, improving customer experience and company brand.
  • Provides a central location to communicate or get information about any incident on the network, acting as a central control for communications with the ability to deliver the status of the network as a whole or a portion of it at any given time.
  • The NOC offers a solution that can guarantee service levels and availability for the network, since all deviations are detected in a timely manner and fixed as soon as possible, minimizing response times, and maximizing the element availability.
  • On high impacting events, the NOC becomes the command and control center for the situation, including all the reporting and the knowledge of the event.

Additionally, the NaaS operators NOC must consider the implication of having as customers one or several MNOs, each of them with their respective NOCs. The coordination between the different NOCs will generate additional inputs and steps on the NOC processes, which will be discussed in section 4.

Coordination must be established between the NOC entities. The collaboration can be implemented at different levels such as:

  1. Ticketing
  2. Fault management
  3. Billing / revenue reconciliation
  4. Performance monitoring

These levels of collaboration need to be established between the NaaS operator and the customer MNOs to efficiently combine their efforts. 2.2 NOC Organization

From a functional view, the NOC includes the following services:

  • Network surveillance
  • Fault management (tier 1 and tier 2)
  • Performance management
  • OSS and tools management
  • Access management
  • Field dispatch

These functions will be covered in-depth on Section 3 of this module, where appropriate guidance will be provided for the NaaS operator to define the structure that best matches the intended scope of the organization.

The functions above are integrated into processes, which are executed by the NOC. From a process view, the NOC includes the following responsibilities:

  1. Event management
  2. Incident management
  3. Problem management
  4. Change management

These processes will be covered in Section 4 of this module. The NaaS operator will be presented with alternatives to customize the processes according to their requirements and capabilities. Its important to consider that the NaaS operator processes will have inputs and outputs from/to NOCs from customer MNOs. These collaborations imply variations to the processes, based on the business and operational model in addition to the responsibilitys division between the NaaS and other MNOs. Guidance to define the business and operational model along with the responsibility split is provided in section 6.

The diverse teams working within the NOC organization coordinate their efforts in order to prevent or minimize any unplanned interruption on the network, or a reduction/degradation in the quality of the service delivered to the end-user. The main roles and responsibilities are summarized below:

  • Network surveillance is recorded by the fault management tier 1 team and escalated to the fault management tier 2 team if applicable to fix the event at hand.
  • Access management is invoked when field assistance is needed and access to premises is required.
  • Incident managers supervise all incident life cycles and follow up on high-impact incidents until resolution.
  • Problem managers investigate the root cause of high impact or recurring incidents.
  • Change managers are responsible for network configuration and the coordination and implementation of all changes in the network
  • Performance managers overview performance or quality degradation problems in the network
  • The OSS & tools team supervises all the tools used within the network

This module will enable NaaS operators to examine the different scenarios and to choose which functions could be merged in their operation according to their scope.

3 NOC Functions

This section defines the NOC from a functional level, focusing on its vital aspects and the configuration possibilities for the NaaS operator.


3.1 NOC Functions Overview

In the following subsections, the description for each function of the NOC is presented along with an analysis of the key metrics and the relevance/need for each function.

3.1.1 Network Surveillance

Network surveillance is the first and most vital function that is supported by a NOC. NOC staff performs continuous monitoring of all the network elements, using available tools like a network monitoring system (see Network Monitoring Architecture module) or via the different element management systems (EMS) provided by equipment vendors. In addition, the NaaS Operator must grant limited access to these systems to the MNO NOCs to monitor only the elements of the network related to their users.

For those network or performance alarms detected, NOC staff will conduct preliminary filtering (such as correlation, alarms due to planned work, etc.), open the corresponding trouble ticket (TT), monitor the alarm warnings, categorize/prioritize the alert severity and handle the problem escalation according to defined procedures. During this stage, a constant communication between the tenant NOCs and the NaaS operator NOC will lead to a better categorization, filtering and avoidance of unnecessary TT.

Network surveillance function typically includes the following activities:

  1. Real-time alarm monitoring of the network health status
  2. Real-time alarm monitoring of all alarms (available via EMS/UNMS)
  3. Alarm monitoring of the network performance and the network service stability
  4. Isolate network alarm or event to the appropriate group for resolution
  5. Filter alarms resulting from scheduled events or maintenance work
  6. Combine and group alarms (correlated alarms) with common fault reasons
  7. Record and collect fault information and initiate trouble ticket request to fault management
  8. Initiate recovery actions and notification procedures
  9. Escalate TTs to NOC second level support or any other area involved, according to the defined service level
  10. Other contractors and third-party escalation
  11. Liaise with customer NOCs for fault- and performance-related problems reporting (for example, a problem may affect only one MNO so the combined reporting may lead to the isolation of the impact).
  12. Interface with external service providers or interconnect carrier/operator (for example, leased line provider, fixed network or international signaling carrier) for fault-related reporting

Network surveillance is the most critical function in the NOC. Since this is the function that monitors incoming alarms and events on the network, it is the first-point entry for network status and enables responses in a timely manner which translates into an overall improvement of restoration times.

Typical metrics used to evaluate the performance of this function are:

  • Percentage of acknowledged alarms vs total alarms Measures the number of alarms the NOC receives and positively identifies.
  • Percentage of alarms per MNO vs total alarms Measures the number of alarms the NOC receives and only affects a specific MNO.
  • Maintenance-related alarms positive identification Measures that all alarms identified as being part of maintenance/planned work were correlated in a correct manner.
  • Alarm response time Measures the time it takes the NOC to identify and respond to an alarm, obtaining the percentage of alarms with response time within a targeted and predefined time.

3.1.2 Fault Management Tier 1

Fault management (tier 1) function includes fault analysis, first-level corrective maintenance, End-to-End (E2E) fault management and incident management. The purpose is to filter and solve most of the cases without on-site intervention or additional resources within the timeframe specified by service levels.

Fault management (Tier 1) functions include the following tasks:

  1. Acknowledge tickets/reports from network surveillance, preventive maintenance, performance management
  2. Classify the fault severity to meet the service level requirements. Severities may be defined as critical / major / medium / minor
  3. Conduct tier 1 fault isolation, diagnosis and solution on the impacted equipment
  4. TT correlation with the existing ones or creation of a new one
  5. Escalate or assign the TT
  6. Remote corrective actions if the problem can be tackled without on-site intervention
  7. When on-site activities are required, escalate to field maintenance team to dispatch workforce
  8. For complex faults, escalate the TT to second level support (tier 2)
  9. Keep track of the TT status and perform management escalation if not resolved within the agreed time frame
  10. Collaboration for alarm correlation, initial troubleshooting, and corrective activities with involved MNO customers (if applicable) besides national and international interconnection partners/carriers and external service providers

It is imperative that the NaaS operator NOC and the NOCs from customer MNOs have constant communication when issuing TT to avoid duplicity and waste of technical resources. The best approach is through a direct connection of the ticketing systems of the respective NOCs, but since compatibility may be an issue, a primitive method could be used such as phone calls to open TT from customer to NaaS Operator.

The fault management function is the second most vital function within a NOC. The group that executes this function assures that alarms that were acknowledged in the previous step are worked and eventually resolved, contributing to the overall network performance and as a result, having an impact on customer satisfaction.

The most common metrics used to evaluate this function are:

  • Trouble ticket opening Measures the time in which a ticket is opened, usually measured against each severity category (critical, major, medium, minor). This can also be categorized per MNO, depending on the opener.
  • Effective fault severity identification Measures that tickets are opened with the correct severity according to predefined definitions for categorization.
  • Escalation time Measures the average time spent by the fault management tier 1 team on initial fault isolation and diagnosis before escalating the ticket to the corresponding fix agent. This can also be categorized per MNO, depending on the TT opener.
  • First time right Measures the percentage of tickets that were resolved by the first fix agent the ticket was escalated to. This can also be categorized per MNO, depending on the TT opener.

Similarly, it is recommended that the fault management systems from the NaaS operator and customer MNOs work together or be interconnected to minimize communication time. One implementation option is to connect the fault management systems from the NaaS operator and customer MNOs using open interfaces. In this case, the NaaS operator must limit the MNOs access to prevent exposing the complete network metrics or KPIs due to privacy issues.

3.1.3 Fault Management Tier 2

Fault management tier 2 resolves the network faults escalated by fault management tier 1 or first-line maintenance teams also known as field technicians. If a fault cannot be solved by tier 2 staff, it will be escalated to tier 3 support which for most NaaS operators will consist of requesting direct support from equipment vendors.

As the tenant NOCs may have more significant experience in the troubleshooting for mobile networks, an alternative is to rely on the tier 2 and/or tier 3 from them. This would decrease the required budget for the NaaS operator NOC and shorten the implementation time. However, an agreement must be reached first with customer MNOs. This tactic will be further reviewed in sections 6.4 and 6.5.

The main activities are:

  1. Provide multi-vendor second-level system support with product expertise to resolve fault issues escalated from the first level after the proper diagnosis
  2. Carry out remote fault investigation and solution, if possible
  3. Escalation of TTs to tier 3 support
  4. Solution verification after a network problem is resolved to confirm that the network is back to normal status
  5. Proactively identify chronic or potentially hazardous network conditions through its monitoring tools and propose actions to be taken to prevent future incidents
  6. Constant communication with tenant NOCs to coordinate troubleshooting and corrective actions to resolve network incidents

This specific function is assigned to a group of people whose expertise is broader and deeper than the fault management tier 1 team. This team usually works on cases that the previous team was not able to diagnose, escalates cases to tier 3 support (vendor support) and supervises the diagnosis, troubleshooting and resolution of high severity scenarios.

Having a fault management tier 2 team helps the organization by lowering resolution times for complex cases. The absence of this team implies that cases need to be escalated to vendor support directly by the fault management tier 1 team, thus increasing the time and cost to resolve the incident.

Generally used metrics to evaluate this function are:

  • Percentage of resolved tickets Measures the number of tickets that were resolved within the team without escalating to a different fix agent. This should also be categorized per MNO.
  • Ticket volume escalated to 3rd level support Measures the number of tickets that were escalated to vendor support for resolution. This can also be categorized per MNO.

3.1.4 Performance Management

This function consists of identifying performance-related issues for network elements and ensuring they are correctly prioritized and managed until proper resolution. Performance management includes the analysis and reporting of network quality measures.

The main activities include:

  1. Collection and analysis of network performance data from the EMSes or any other applicable source
  2. Creation of performance and traffic reports
  3. Detection of problematic elements
  4. Troubleshooting of those performance issues detected

The performance management team works on performance-related scenarios that are not related to break/fix cases. This function frequently works with engineering/optimization teams to verify, validate, and resolve performance-related issues. These issues are categorized and treated according to the SLA agreed with the affected MNOs (for example, response times, escalation times and priorities). When possible, it is a good practice to target the tightest agreed SLA in the network; in any case, network KPIs should be reported on a per-MNO basis.

The most common metrics used to evaluate this function are:

  • Ticket volume Measures the number of tickets that are being created over a specific pre-established period. This metric should also be categorized per MNO.
  • Ticket categorization Measures the percentage of tickets that are created against common categories: accessibility (RRC, ERAB), latency, retainability (dropped call, blocked call), mobility (handover)

3.1.5 OSS & Tools Management

OSS & tools team is the administrative entity for all OSS, EMS and NOC tools that are deployed for network management. OSS management includes the analysis and reporting of OSS quality measures.

The main activities include:

  1. User management
  2. Software and data management
  3. OSS administration
  4. OSS fault management
  5. System backup management

The OSS & tools function aids the different functions of the NOC with daily tasks as user administration which includes account or permission details, access rights OSS management including routine maintenance, health checks, system backups, system upgrades (for all EMS being supervised on the network). These tasks are accomplished faster by having a local team versus opening tickets against the vendors for assistance.

According to the collaboration levels to be implemented (see section 6.2), privilege access to different files/systems may be granted to tenant NOCs. The first consideration should be based on the permissions that the NaaS operator will grant to the customer MNOs to access their systems. In a similar vein, the NaaS operator NOC would require permission to access the systems of the tenant NOCs so the level of access must be conceded accordingly. These considerations should be handled by the OSS & tools management team as coordinator.

The collaboration strategy covers four areas: ticketing, fault management, performance management and billing & revenue reconciliation (further details in section 6.2). For these areas, the degree of privileges can vary from one MNO to another. If systems are interconnected usually through APIs or standardized interfaces the OSS & Tools team will oversee the level of access of tenant NOCs to access only to network data or equipment related to a specific MNO or its customers. Customer MNOs must not be capable of accessing the complete information of the network since this may cause privacy issues.

The most often used metrics to evaluate this function are:

  • User requests volume Measures the number of requests received to add/change/delete users or modify rights/permissions
  • Fault management ticket volume Measures the number of tickets being resolved by the OSS & tools team
  • Percentage of resolved tickets Measures the number of tickets that were resolved within the team without escalating to the vendor or the tier 2 customer MNO NOC (depending on the business model). This metric should also be categorized per MNO.

3.1.6 Access Management

Access management is responsible for site access requests and follow up.

Activities performed by this team include:

  1. Access request admission
  2. Access submission to tower companies/landlords
  3. Check-in and check-out validations
  4. Access escalations

The access management function ensures that access to the different sites in the network will be ready when needed by the field technicians or service providers. This mitigates the burden for the field management team as this function is normally associated with them if the NOC does not have this team within their structure. In the case that the field technicians from the NaaS Operator need access to a customer MNO facility, the access management function will also be in charge of coordinating with tenant NOCs the access to those sites (e.g., installing equipment on an MNO facility for interconnection).

Typically used metrics to evaluate the performance of this function are:

  • Site access requests Measures the number of access requests managed by the team, often broken up by field tech requests and third-party requests (e.g., customer MNO requests).
  • Access escalations Measures the number and level of escalations performed by the team to gain access to sites.

3.1.7 Field Dispatch

Field dispatch covers the coordination for on-site interventions that are executed on the network.

Activities performed include:

  1. Dispatch of the work order
  2. Preparation of the field intervention - for example: spare parts collection and site access authorization
  3. Execution of the work order
  4. Reporting the outcome to the requestor (and the return of the spare parts, if applicable)

The field dispatch function is another critical function peripheral to a NOC as the team resolves all on-site interventions, including both corrective and preventive maintenance tasks. This specific function will be covered in far more detail in the Field Force Management module.


3.2 NOC Functions Selection & Customization.

This section examines the possible scenarios for the deployment of functions on the NOC organization, presenting alternatives to choose from, based on the NaaS operators scope and needs.

Below, the different options to customize the functions for each team in the NOC are examined.

3.2.1 NOC Tier 1 Setup

As stated before, the tier 1 team is the first point of contact when an issue in the network arises. However, the configuration of functions for this team can vary based on the NaaS operator requirements and constraints. The sections below, provide an overview and guidance for NaaS operators to select the option that best suits their needs.

3.2.1.1 Network Surveillance + Fault Management Scenario

The aggregation of network surveillance and fault management is one of the most common scenarios at the tier 1 level. Since network surveillance and fault management tier 1 go hand-by-hand, these two functions are usually met by one single instance which is accommodated into a NOC tier 1 level. This group of people will both perform real-time monitoring of all alarms, create trouble tickets, perform first-level analysis and diagnosis, and escalate the ticket to the corresponding fix agent if unable to resolve.

The group will also be responsible for all email and phone call communications regarding the tier 1 team, including exchanges for site conditions, active alarms, and network alarm status.

NaaS operators should select this option if a high volume of alarms/tickets is not expected, or if they wish to keep both functions tied to a single team in order for the tier 1 team to have a complete picture of both alarms and trouble tickets.

3.2.1.2 Region Segmentation Scenario

Another common setup revolves around aggregating the access management and field dispatch functions into the tier 1 team as well. This can prove effective when the NOC is monitoring a small network (or if network monitoring is broken up into different regions, each one covered by a small team of engineers).

In this scenario, the tier 1 team will also be in charge of executing field dispatches (if necessary) and making the necessary adjustments to make sure that there will be access to the site for the field technicians.

As mentioned before, this option is best suited for region-based monitoring or for small networks, as the increase in functions makes them aware of all details for their respective sites, but in turn, the overhead associated with this setup may challenge the team.

3.2.1.3 Domain Segmentation Scenario

Another common scenario is to have the NOC tier 1 team segmented into the different network domains, typically RAN, TX and CORE, especially if the alarm volume expected will be high. As such, each team monitors and creates trouble tickets for the alarms coming in from the different network elements in each domain.

For instance, the RAN tier 1 team monitoring a 4G network would receive alarms from the eNBs and the cell site routers, working closely with site-level fix agents, like field technicians covering such sites, tower companies, power companies and fiber companies. The TX tier 1 team would receive alarms from the IP aggregation and backbone routers, working with field technicians, power companies and fiber service providers. Finally, the CORE tier 1 team would receive alarms coming from the EPC network elements like the MME, SGW, PGW, etc.

This setup is more complex than the previous one, as all tier 1 teams must be in constant communication and coordination to successfully correlate alarms to incidents within the network. Some operators choose to have only two tier 1 teams: access (including all RAN and aggregation nodes) and CORE. These segmented scenarios make more sense to operators with medium-to-large site presence and can be combined with the previous region segmentation mentioned earlier for better results in larger, nation-wide networks.

3.2.1.4 Enhanced Tier 1 Scenario

Finally, one less common setup is incorporating fault management tier 2 functions to the NOC tier 1 team in addition to the previously mentioned functions. This is referred to as a tier 1.5 team, or enhanced tier 1 team.

This option may be taken for budget constraints as there would only be one team overseeing, analyzing, and processing all activities within the network, escalating complex cases directly to the vendors. This setup requires engineers with a higher level of expertise which must be taken into consideration. This can be an option for small networks with more relaxed service level agreements, as cases escalated to vendors may extend resolution times.

3.2.2 NOC Tier 2 Setup

As tier 2 teams are more specialized with equipment/technology, they are regularly set up within domains like RAN, TX and CORE. In addition, the following function bundles are assigned to the Tier 2 Team.

This option is adopted by NOCs in which Operators do not have network performance set as a priority. As such, Network Performance is not monitored in real-time and is only escalated to the NOC tier 2 team via end-customer reports. 3.2.3 NOC Leadership

NOC leadership duties include NOC planning, controlling, steering, and reporting all aspects of NOC activities; management of NOC organization and NOC staffing; delegation and empowerment of NOC managers and team members. Within those duties, the following main activities are performed:

  1. Key performance indicators (KPI) measurement
  2. KPI and SLA reporting
  3. Shift management

Figure 4 shows the most common structure for NOC Leadership, which will be explained in detail in the following paragraphs:

Figure 4 — Network Operations Leadership View

Each NOC group is usually supervised by a manager who delivers guidance to the entire team and serves as the point of contact (POC) for all those team requirements or escalations. Depending on the setup that has been chosen, leaders for network surveillance, fault management tier 1, fault management tier 2, performance management, OSS & tools management, access management, incident management and field dispatch may exist; or a manager for the aggregated teams may be in place (e.g., NOC tier 1 manager, NOC tier 2 manager). The entire team is supervised by a NOC head, who acts as the ultimate responsible for the NOC operations.

Another common practice in medium-to-large NOC organizations is to have a manager per domain, specifically at the tier 2 level. This decision is based on the expertise that is needed for such teams and to have a direct escalation path available for specific-domain scenarios that may be present on the network, thus aiding for faster resolutions since the leader can provide directions for the team and readjusting priorities as needed.

Since regardless of the grouping decisions the tier 1 team is always one of the biggest groups, team leaders are regularly set as part of the structure (having at least one team leader per shift). These people are trained engineers whose expertise and process knowledge, supported by leadership empowerment, enables them to make controlled operative decisions during their shift in absence of the teams manager.

Further guidance on the configuration of the NOC leadership team is provided in section 6.4.


4 NOC Processes

This section presents the core processes followed by the NOC including E2E process flows, key elements, roles and responsibilities and the most common metrics used to measure each process success. The section also presents guidance for the operators NOC processes customization.


4.1 NOC Processes Analysis

The following subsections analyze each process executed at the NOC, including rationale, common KPIs and roles associated with each process.

4.1.1 Event Management

An event is regarded as a change of state that has significance for the management of a resource or service. It can also mean an alert or notification created by any service, resource or monitoring tool.

Events typically require operations staff to take actions, and often lead to incidents being logged. The event is only recognized as an incident at the end of the event management process.

4.1.2 Incident Management

To understand the incident management process, the following definitions are required:

  1. Incident Unplanned interruption to the NaaS operators service, or a reduction in the quality of their service. An incident can also include failure within a system that has not yet impacted the NaaS operators service. An example would be a loss of functionality that has switched over to a back-up system.
  2. High impact, or potentially high impact incident Incident that requires a response that is above and beyond what is given to normal incidents. Typically, these incidents require cross-function or cross-department coordination, management escalation, the mobilization of additional resources, and increased communications.
  3. Extraordinary impact incident Incident characterized as a crisis, emergency outage, or priority zero problem. It is an event in the operational phase of the network that has a severe impact on customer service and is assumed to be or has already been escalated within the NaaS operators organization.

  • Cross technology incident Percent of incidents that involved several technologies. An example would be a combined investigation effort required with engineers from RAN and CORE.
  • Diagnosis Accuracy Percent of tickets that were assigned to the correct team.

4.1.3 Problem Management

To understand the Incident Management process, the following definitions are required:

  1. Problem Underlying cause of one or more incidents, either existing or potential. Problems are sometimes identified because of multiple incidents that exhibit common symptoms. They can also be identified from a single significant incident for which the cause is unknown, but the impact is high.
  2. Root cause analysis (RCA) Process of analyzing and determining the underlying cause of an incident or series of incidents. Identifying the underlying cause helps to identify permanent solutions for problems in the network.
  3. Workaround Interim solution, that reduces or eliminates the impact of an incident or problem for which a full resolution is not yet available (e.g., restarting a failed network element). Workarounds for problems are commonly documented in known error records.
  4. Problem solution Long-term solution to a problem to prevent recurrence of incidents.
  5. Known error Condition identified by the successful diagnosis of the root cause of a problem. Successful diagnosis converts an unknown error to an error whose root cause is known. Problem management identifies and manages known errors throughout their lifecycle. Known errors may also be identified by developers or suppliers.
  6. Known error database (KEDB) Database that allows for storage of previous knowledge of incidents and problems and how they were overcome to allow quicker diagnosis and resolution if they reoccur.

4.1.4 Change Management

A network change refers to the addition, modification, or removal of any network infrastructure network element. This includes changes to hardware, software, configuration, and database. First, some concepts that are important to the change management process are provided:

a) A change request is a request for a change on the network, which can be accepted or rejected.

b) Changes are often categorized by their nature:

i. Standard change refers to change with low risk and no service impact. Standard changes are pre-authorized. The processes and methods of procedures (MOP) are clearly defined and are proven. The standard changes are often requested on a regular basis.

ii. Normal change refers to change requiring an approval. Normal change type encompasses all changes except the standard changes, the emergency changes and the planned works.

iii. An emergency change is used when immediate action is required in order to restore the customer services or to prevent imminent failures, without time to follow the normal change process.

iv. Planned work is any activity scheduled by a third party on equipment outside the NaaS operator network that could have an impact on services.

c) Changes are commonly categorized by their complexity as well:

i. Single change is a change that is to be implemented by a single implementer or in a single place or in a single network equipment type. It requires minimal coordination.

ii. Complex change refers to change to be implemented by several implementers or in several places or several network equipment types. Synchronization between the implementation phases is mandatory.

    3) The first decision point for the change coordinator is made based on whether a change is a normal change or a standard change.

    4) The standard change is pre-approved and will go directly to the next process step: prepare change.

    5) The normal change must first undergo an administrative review by the change coordinator (a), and a technical review by the subject matter expert (b) before it is authorized.

    6) When the review process is complete, the change goes to the change advisory board (CAB) or the emergency change advisory board (E-CAB). These boards may consist of a group of people, or a single person.

    The CAB/E-CAB should be in constant communication with their customer MNOs that can be affected by the change. An additional coordination with the affected MNO may be required if the change will impact on the service provided by the NaaS operator.

    Reciprocally, the tenant NOCs must notify of changes in their networks that could lead to outages or any impact on the NaaS operator network or services. For changes with major impact a formal notification and coordination with the NaaS operator may be necessary as well.

    In parallel with the involved tenant NOC, the CAB/E-CAB performs their review and evaluates the planning details to determine if theres any conflict with other changes already approved and planned. The board renders a decision to either reject the change and stop the process or approve the change and pass it along to the next step: prepare change. The involved tenant NOCs may request a delay in the activities if the impact overlaps with their own activities.

    7) In prepare change, both the change coordinator and the change implementer of NaaS NOC and tenant NOCs take care of the last preparations and adjust the timing between a normal change and a standard change. The next decision point is based on whether the change is a simple change or a complex change.

    8) The simple change goes directly into the implementation and testing phase (a). The complex change requires coordination of NaaS and MNO NOCs (b), which is the responsibility of the change coordinator. As the change coordinator is working, the change implementer is operating in parallel to perform implementation and testing activities.

    9) Throughout the implementation process, the change implementer needs to be in contact with the network monitoring team in the NOC. The network monitoring team should be in contact with their counterparts in the tenant NOCs. This contact is necessary to ask the fault management tier 1 engineer if change implementation can begin.

    During the change implementation activities, there is the possibility of an unforeseen issue, error, or incident that can have an impact on the planned work underway, or even an outage caused by the change itself. In any of these cases, the NOC is contacted for a go ahead, followed by implementation and testing.

    For example, if the change implementer loses track of time, the network monitoring team should contact the change implementer when there is only a predefined number of minutes left of the available maintenance window. They can discuss if the change implementation can finish on time or if a rollback is necessary to ensure the work remains within the requested maintenance window.

    The NOC does have the authority to recommend a rollback if there is a reason to prevent any further damage or service affecting issues in the network. Also, the NOC has the authority to request the status of the activities on customer MNOs networks to correlate with the status of its own network. If the impact if bigger than expected, the NaaS Operator NOC can make a formal rollback request to the corresponding tenant NOC.

    10) The two possible conditions of change implementation and testing are: successful or not successful with a rollback. In either case, the results of the change are reviewed by the change manager, change coordinator and change implementer.

    11) The next point is based on what kind of change took place. There may have been changes to the network, or new equipment may have been installed. There may have been no change except for replacing an existing card with the same type of card. In this case, the next step is reporting and closing.

    Once the updates in documentation to the required system are performed (asset and configuration database), the process moves to the last step, which is preparing the implementation report and closing the change ticket.

    Typical KPIs used to measure the change management process are:

    ● On-Time Change Complete Percentage of change requests that are delivered against the planned or agreed-upon committed date.

    ● Change causing an incident Percentage of changes that caused an incident. The incident in this case is any outage or service interruption that is not documented and approved by the change advisory board.

    ● Change volume Total number of changes approved in each month. Its useful for monitoring the volume of changes implemented in a network.

    ● Emergency Change Rate. Percentage of changes classified as emergency against the total changes implemented in the network. This is an important KPI for process performance measurements. A higher count of emergency changes represents a more unstable network, where incident restoration requires emergency change. This can also be a result of poor change management process leading to unplanned emergency changes.


    4.2 NOC Process Selection & Configuration

    Now that the NOC processes have been analyzed the NaaS Operator must select the processes to be implemented based on the NOC scope and NaaS operator capabilities or requirements.

    Event management, incident management and change management are mandatory processes for a NOC operation. Still, there are three process adjustments that may be considered by the NaaS operator:

    ● Incident Management

    If budget constraints call for headcount reduction, or if the operator rules out coordination and communication activities as mandatory during high impact scenarios, the incident manager may be removed from the overall process.

    In this scenario, a member from the fault management tier 2 team will be in charge of the incident coordination and will serve as the POC for the duration of the event. The operator may decide that for escalations, awareness, and directions on high-impact incidents the shift leader (if applicable), or the domain manager, or even the NOC manager may be requested to attend.

    Consider that in the absence of an Incident Manager, communications during a high impact scenario may not formally exist, and as such, the different teams working the case must make a conscious effort to document the trouble ticket to the best of their abilities, as it is often required to review all actions taken during troubleshooting for improvement purposes.

    ● Problem Management

    A very common practice, at least at the early stages of NOC implementations, is to leave the problem management process out from the NOC set of processes.

    Problem management is a powerful process that enables the operator to reduce the number of recurring scenarios, to mitigate the impact for future events or to enhance the end-customer experience by optimizing the network in certain aspects. Still, operators may conclude that at early stages or in general focus will be set on network availability alone, and that scope will be limited to event detection and restoral, and not on enhancement opportunities.

    On such organizations, the closest existing approach to problem management is performed by the fault management tier 2 team, and usually triggered only by request, generally from a high-visibility escalation. The process may be executed at some extent, generally without a knowledge base properly set, and as such problems detected in the network may have longer resolution times compared to a fully implemented problem management organization.

    ● Change Management

    In the Change management process, the operator may judge that two different entities are not relevant for the process, as the change coordinator and the change manager may be comprised of a single entity within the NOC structure.

    On this setup, the coordinator or the manager will inherit the activities performed by the other entity within the change management process, leaving a single POC for all change-related queries.

    Another option followed by some operators is to leave out both the coordinator and the manager out of the equation, and as such assignment of responsibilities are set internally within the change engineers structure, or assigned to the NOC tier 2 team, in which case assignment per domain is executed for change request review, approval and follow up.

3.1.2 Corrective Maintenance.

Corrective Maintenance can be triggered by the preventive inspections which detect that corrective actions are required and will require service interruption such as cabling replacement. However corrective actions are triggered as well in case of unexpected failures. In this case, the Dispatcher/ Field Manager releases a work order which Field Technicians must review and understand before departure to the site. The following subsections include a high-level overview of the key tasks that the field maintenance team may perform before, during and after corrective actions.

3.1.2.1 Corrective Maintenance on Antenna Systems

Corrective interventions in antenna systems are commonly triggered when an alarm is detected by the NOC. Field technicians must analyze the Antenna System elements looking for faulty components. The best practice is to start looking for faults that require lower impact repairs, considering hardware replacements as the last option.

Hardware replacement in the Antenna system requires special attention since it may require the use of a pulley and winch to replace the Radio Units, Antennas or Structural supports. Additionally, its recommended that at least three people work together to perform a radio or antenna replacement. Field Management must ensure the availability of these resources.

NaaS Operators may consider the following method to perform any Corrective Intervention in the Cell Site Antenna System:

Pre-requisites

  • Field Technicians/Field Management must ensure that a winch, pulley, rope and the required tools are available to perform the Antenna Systems Maintenance.
  • Field Management must ensure that at least three qualified technicians able to perform height works are available.
  • Field Technicians must prepare their Personal Protective equipment, including the necessary equipment to work at heights.
  • The model of the faulty Radio Unit, Antenna, Microwave Dish or Structural Supports are confirmed, and the New Equipment of the same model is ready.

Procedure

  1. Check if the faulty Radio unit breaker is switched on and has no damage. In case it is switched off, turn on the switch which should remain on. In the case of automatic switch off, do not turn it on again.
  2. Check if the Power Cables are properly connected to the Radio units and Power Distribution Units, and they are not burned. The Power cable should not have any presence of damage. Replace the connectors or cable if it is necessary.
  3. If power cables and breakers are in good condition, measure the voltage output from the PDU, which should be within the operative voltage, replace rectifiers in case voltage are not within the operative voltage ranges, please see section 3.1.2.3 Corrective Maintenance on the Power System.
  4. Check that the fibers are not damaged and optic connectors are clean in baseband and radio side. Replace the fiber if it is necessary.
  5. Check if the RF jumpers are properly tightened at the torque specified by the Radio Unit vendor if its possible to perform a VSWR test after RF cable adjustment. The VSWR should be within the limits specified by vendors. Replace the RF cable if its necessary.
  6. If it is required to replace the Radio equipment, Antennas and/or Mounting Structures Field technicians must follow the instructions provided by the vendors of each equipment.

Closing Tasks

  • After the corrective actions are performed, Field technicians must contact NOC to verify the Cell site status and clear remaining alarms.
  • Once alarms are cleared Field Technicians must prepare the report detailing the Spare Parts used.

3.1.2.2 Corrective Maintenance on Baseband and Transmission Equipment.

When corrective actions are triggered to maintain the baseband or transmission equipment most likely the site will be out of service. Field technicians must perform certain actions before considering hardware replacement, below there are some considerations for corrective maintenance on Baseband and Transmission Equipment.

Pre-requisites

  • Field technicians must ensure that there is a backup configuration file of the baseband and transmission configuration, this procedure may be performed by the NOC, storing the configuration file in the O&M system or locally by the Field technicians. The best practice is to have the backup file in both locations.
  • The model of the faulty board or chassis is confirmed, and the New Equipment of the same model is ready

Procedure

  1. Check if Baseband and Transmission breakers are turned on and have no damage. In case it is switched off, turn on the switch which should remain on. In the case of automatic switch off, do not turn it on again.
  2. Check cable connection between transmission equipment and baseband. Replace the cable if necessary.
  3. Check the baseband transmission status from the local maintenance port this process should be detailed in vendor documentation. In case transmission is not available Field Technician must notify the NOC.
  4. Check if the Power Cables are properly connected to the baseband and transmission equipment, and connectors are not burned. The Power cable should not have any presence of damage. Replace the connectors or cable if it is necessary.
  5. If power cables and breakers are in good condition, measure the voltage output from the PDU, which should be within the operative voltage, replace rectifiers in case voltage are not within the operative voltage ranges please see section 3.1.2.3 Corrective Maintenance on the Power System.
  6. If PDUs and rectifiers are working properly a board replacement should be considered.
  7. Ensure that a Backup file is safe, Field technicians may power-off the base station, disconnect the cables from the damaged board always having a record of a connection map.
  8. Field technicians must replace the damaged board with the new one, following the vendor documentation, then reconnect all fibers and cables in the same way that was before the replacement. NOC personnel must ensure the configuration is the same after the replacement.

Closing Tasks

  • After the replacement Field technicians must contact the NOC to verify the Cell site status and clear remaining alarms.

The same process applies for Transmission equipment, including switches, cell-routers, IDU or VSAT modems.

In case that it is required to replace fan modules within the Baseband/transmission equipment or cabinets it is recommendable to turn off the equipment. However, some vendors detail in their documentation that the fans can be replaced without interrupting the service if the baseband/transmission equipment is located in controlled environments that do not exceed a certain temperature and the replacement procedure dont exceed 20 minutes.

3.1.2.3 Corrective Maintenance on the Power System

In order to perform any corrective action within the power system will be required to disconnect the main power supply to protect the technicians and the cell site equipment, below are some recommendations that NaaS operators may consider to include them in their operational procedures:

Prerequisites

  • The model of the faulty Power Equipment is confirmed, and the New Equipment of the same model is ready.
  • Field technicians must ensure that the hardware version is consistent with that of the faulty Power Equipment.
  • The tools and materials, such as an ESD wrist strap or a pair of ESD gloves, Phillips screwdriver, ESD box or bag, and key to the cabinet door are ready.
  • Field technicians must ensure that there is a backup configuration file of the baseband and transmission configuration, this procedure may be performed by the NOC, storing the configuration file in the O&M system or locally by the Field technicians. The best practice is to have the backup file in both locations.

Procedure

  1. Check if the main input switch is turned on and have no damage. In case it is switched off, turn on the switch which should remain on. In the case of automatic switch off, do not turn it on again.
  2. Check the main switch voltage, notify NOC /Fieldwork Management if voltage measurement is not within the operative range or there is no power.
  3. Check Rectifiers and Power Distribution Units are properly connected to the Power System.
  4. Disconnect the cables connected to the equipment maintaining a connection map at hand.
  5. Replace the Damaged Equipment by the new one.
  6. Connect all the power cables ensuring that the connections are the same as it was before the replacement using the connection map.

Closing Tasks

  • After the replacement Field technicians must contact NOC to verify the Cell site status and clear all remaining alarms.

3.1.2.4 Corrective Maintenance on Facilities and Shelter Environment

Facilities corrective interventions may be triggered by a preventive maintenance, or post-checks during other corrective interventions.

Facilities Maintenance requires a set of tools and materials that NaaS operators may not have in their warehouse, additionally it is not expected that field technicians have the required skills to repair fences, shelters or towers. The recommended strategy for Facilities Maintenance is to hire maintenance services from the construction companies or Contractors. Field Management must keep track of the companies that provide these services, oversee the process, and ensure that the required maintenance is performed.


3.2 Maintenance Implementation

In order to standardize the Maintenance interventions in their cell sites, NaaS Operators may consider a Maintenance Manual which Field Technicians and Management must follow. The elaboration of Maintenance Manuals ensure that a process is followed, guaranteeing a quality intervention, diminishing risks, and preventing the creation of more issues during an intervention. Additionally, Maintenance Interventions should be reported to keep track of maintenance history, which enhances the health visibility of each piece of equipment. This section provides guidelines to elaborate the Maintenance Manuals and the Maintenance Reports, to implement maintenance operations into the NaaS Networks.

3.2.1 Field Maintenance Manual

The Field Maintenance Manual is a document that includes the process that the Field Maintenance team must follow in order to perform any maintenance intervention.

The structure of this document must be a step-by-step guide for preventive and corrective maintenance. NaaS Operators may consider as a reference, the content of Sections 2, Sections 3.1.1 and Section 3.1.2 to elaborate them. Please note that the actual maintenance and replacement procedures are detailed by the vendor of each equipment as well as the required tools.

The Maintenance Manual must contain the following outline:

  1. Process Summary
  2. Maintenance Pre-work
  3. Health & Safety Measures
  4. Preventive Maintenance Instructions
  5. Corrective Maintenance Instructions

This module provides a Maintenance Manual template for Antenna System, Baseband/Transmission Equipment, and Power system in order to be adapted by NaaS Operators. The following sections present an analysis of the Maintenance Manual Content with the structure previously mentioned, including examples for Preventive and Corrective Maintenance instructions.

3.2.1.1 Process Summary

The Maintenance Manual must provide a summary of the Maintenance Process including a process diagram that covers from Work Order Dispatch and Reception to Reporting. NaaS Operators may take as a reference the content of Section 2 for their Maintenance Manual.

3.2.1.2 Maintenance Pre-work

Maintenance Pre-work consists of a set of validations that Field Technicians must perform before the Maintenance Intervention. NaaS Operators can consider it as a prerequisite before the hands-on tasks and add it to the Maintenance Manual. Below is a list of tasks that a NaaS Operator must consider.

1. Access Request
The Maintenance Manual must include a section that indicates step by step the task that the Field Technician must perform to request access to the site as indicated in the module Field Force Management.

2. Spare Parts and Ancillaries Request
The Maintenance Manual must include the process which Field Technicians must follow to request the required Spare Parts, Ancillaries and Consumables that will be required for the intervention. Specifics regarding Spare Parts Management are covered in the Field Force Management Module.

3. Tools Pre-check
Maintenance Manuals may include a list of tools and testers. This will ensure that installation technicians have all the required tools before they go to the site. NaaS Operators may find the required tools to perform the maintenance interventions in the vendor documentation.

3.2.1.3 Health & Safety Measures

The Maintenance Manual must detail the Health & Safety Measures including a set of tasks to prevent injuries and detail the Personal Protective Equipment that field technicians must wear before the Maintenance Intervention. Field Technicians must consider those tasks a prerequisite for the Preventive or Corrective actions. Below are listed the minimum Health and safety measures to consider before any maintenance intervention:

  • Worksite Analysis and Inspection: Field Technicians must ensure that cell sites have the proper conditions to avoid work accidents. Best practices should not allow work in heights with winds velocity beyond 30Km/h, water, snow or ice accumulated in the tower should be considered a stopper to perform maintenance interventions at height.
  • Emergency Response: Field Technicians must know what to do in case of an Emergency.
  • Personal Protective Equipment: Field technicians must know how to wear properly their Personal Protective Equipment and wear their ID Badge.
  • Electrical Safety: Field Technicians must know the risk of working with electrical equipment.

3.2.2 Maintenance Intervention Report

A Maintenance Report will help NaaS Operators to create a Maintenance History which accurately leads to failure prevention and drives awareness about equipment maintenance.

The Intervention Report can be done through a Dispatch Tool, a Service Management Tool, or storing the reports that are generated manually. The analysis to properly select the Reporting method is deeply analyzed in the Module Field Force Management.

Regardless of the reporting method, a properly reported intervention should include enough elements that prove the intervention was performed correctly. Below the minimum requirements that NaaS operators should consider as part of the Maintenance interventions reports are listed:

  • Site data:
    • Site Name
    • Site Address
    • Site Owner
  • Site access mode: Digi code, key access, etc.
  • The date of the current intervention and last intervention.
  • The work order or ticket number.
  • The name and position who issue and perform the intervention:
    • Issued by: Dispatcher/Field Management.
    • Verified by Dispatcher/Field Management.
    • Approved by Dispatcher/Field Management.
    • Performed by Field Technician Name and Company.
    • Supported by NOC Operator Name.
  • The description of the intervention: Intervention type (Corrective or Preventive), specific constraints.
  • Photographic Evidence of the Overall Cell Site Condition.
  • The Spare Parts used on-site and their respective Serial Numbers.
  • Photographic evidence of the used Spare Parts.
  • The date of the measurements of the batteries and expiration date.
  • Attachments: site map, diagrams.

This module includes a Maintenance Intervention Report Template to be adapted by NaaS Operators in case they decide to store their Maintenance Reports Manually.
